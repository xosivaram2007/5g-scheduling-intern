# Related Work

## A. Traditional Scheduling Approaches
Wireless resource scheduling plays a crucial role in maximizing network performance while ensuring efficient utilization of available radio resources. Before the emergence of artificial intelligence and machine learning techniques, resource allocation in cellular networks primarily relied on heuristic-based scheduling algorithms such as Round Robin (RR), Proportional Fair (PF), and Maximum Throughput (MT). These algorithms use predefined mathematical rules to determine which user equipment (UE) should be allocated radio resources during each transmission interval.

The Round Robin scheduler allocates resources equally among all users in a cyclic manner without considering channel quality. Although this approach ensures fairness and prevents user starvation, it often results in lower overall throughput because users experiencing poor channel conditions receive the same allocation as users with better channel conditions. The Proportional Fair scheduler improves upon this limitation by balancing throughput and fairness. It prioritizes users based on the ratio of their instantaneous achievable data rate to their historical average throughput, allowing the scheduler to exploit favourable channel conditions while maintaining fairness among users. Another commonly used approach is the Maximum Throughput scheduler, which always selects the user with the best channel quality to maximize system throughput. However, this often leads to unfair resource distribution, as users with weaker channel conditions may rarely receive service.

Despite their simplicity and low computational complexity, traditional scheduling algorithms lack adaptability in dynamic 5G environments where network conditions, user mobility, and traffic demands continuously change. These limitations have encouraged researchers to explore intelligent learning-based scheduling techniques capable of making adaptive resource allocation decisions based on the current network state.

## B. Reinforcement Learning Foundations
Reinforcement Learning (RL) is a branch of machine learning in which an intelligent agent learns to make sequential decisions by interacting with an environment. Unlike supervised learning, RL does not require labelled datasets. Instead, the agent observes the current state of the environment, performs an action, and receives a reward that reflects the quality of its decision. Through repeated interactions, the agent learns a policy that maximizes the cumulative reward over time. This trial-and-error learning mechanism makes RL particularly suitable for solving dynamic optimization problems where the environment continuously changes.

The theoretical foundations of reinforcement learning were comprehensively presented by Sutton and Barto [1], who introduced key concepts such as states, actions, rewards, value functions, policies, and exploration-exploitation strategies. Their work established the basis for many modern reinforcement learning algorithms that are widely applied in robotics, autonomous systems, and wireless communications. In wireless resource scheduling, these concepts enable a scheduler to continuously adapt its decisions based on changing network conditions rather than relying on predefined heuristic rules.

As reinforcement learning problems became increasingly complex, traditional tabular methods faced difficulties in handling large state and action spaces. To overcome these limitations, researchers introduced Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL), which combines reinforcement learning with deep neural networks to approximate value functions or policies. Among the most influential DRL algorithms is Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) proposed by Schulman et al. [2]. PPO improves training stability by limiting excessive policy updates while maintaining high sample efficiency, making it suitable for complex decision-making tasks. The success of PPO and other DRL algorithms has encouraged researchers to investigate their application in wireless resource allocation, where adaptive scheduling decisions are essential for meeting the performance requirements of modern 5G networks.

## C. Deep Reinforcement Learning for 5G Resource Scheduling
The rapid evolution of fifth-generation (5G) wireless networks has introduced increasingly complex resource allocation challenges due to higher user density, diverse Quality of Service (QoS) requirements, and continuously changing network conditions. These challenges have motivated researchers to apply Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) to wireless resource scheduling, enabling intelligent agents to learn adaptive scheduling policies directly from network interactions instead of relying on predefined heuristic rules.

Tham et al. [3] presented one of the early studies exploring the application of Deep Reinforcement Learning for resource allocation in Cloud Radio Access Networks (C-RAN). The authors discussed the limitations of conventional optimization techniques, which often make scheduling decisions based only on the current network state and struggle to adapt to highly dynamic environments. Their work highlighted the ability of DRL to solve sequential decision-making problems by considering long-term rewards while handling large state-action spaces through deep neural networks. The paper also identified several research challenges, including computational complexity, scalability, and the need for more efficient learning algorithms for practical 5G deployments.

Kim, Hwang, and Lee [4] proposed a fairness-aware Deep Reinforcement Learning scheduling framework for wireless networks. Their approach was designed to maximize overall system throughput while simultaneously satisfying Quality of Service (QoS) and fairness requirements among users. By carefully designing the reward function to incorporate both throughput and fairness objectives, the proposed scheduler achieved superior performance compared with conventional scheduling methods. Experimental results demonstrated that DRL can effectively balance conflicting performance metrics, making it a promising solution for real-time wireless scheduling. This work is particularly relevant to the present project because throughput and fairness are also used as key evaluation metrics when comparing the Deep Q-Network (DQN) scheduler with traditional scheduling algorithms.

More recently, Xu et al. [5] introduced a dynamic hierarchical reinforcement learning framework for improving the energy efficiency of large-scale 5G base stations in urban environments. Their framework combined Deep Q-Networks (DQN), Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs), attention mechanisms, and multi-agent reinforcement learning to optimize both global base station coordination and local resource management. Extensive simulations demonstrated energy savings of up to 75.6% compared with baseline methods, highlighting the effectiveness of hierarchical reinforcement learning for large-scale wireless systems. This work illustrates the growing trend of integrating multiple deep learning techniques with reinforcement learning to address increasingly complex optimization problems in next-generation communication networks.

Overall, the existing literature demonstrates that Deep Reinforcement Learning provides significant advantages over traditional heuristic scheduling algorithms by improving adaptability, resource utilization, and decision-making under dynamic network conditions. Building upon these studies, the present project implements a Deep Q-Network (DQN)-based scheduler and compares its performance with conventional Round Robin (RR) and Proportional Fair (PF) schedulers using throughput, latency, and fairness metrics. This implementation provides a practical evaluation of DRL-based scheduling while reinforcing the potential of reinforcement learning for intelligent resource allocation in future wireless communication systems.
